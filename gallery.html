                                                                                                          Information of all kinds of hindu festivals








1.Diwali (Deepavali): Known as the Festival of Lights, Diwali is one of the most popular Hindu festivals. It symbolizes the victory of light over darkness and good over evil. During Diwali, people light oil lamps (diyas), decorate their homes, exchange gifts, and enjoy festive sweets and snacks.


2.Holi: Holi is the Festival of Colors and marks the arrival of spring. It involves people throwing colored powders and water at each other, singing and dancing. It's a time for forgiveness, reconciliation, and letting go of past grudges.


3.Navaratri: This nine-night festival is dedicated to the worship of the goddess Durga. It includes fasting, prayer, and traditional dance forms like Garba and Dandiya Raas. The festival culminates with Dussehra, celebrating the victory of Lord Rama over the demon king Ravana.


4.Raksha Bandhan: Raksha Bandhan is a celebration of the bond between brothers and sisters. Sisters tie a protective thread (rakhi) around their brothers' wrists, and brothers, in return, offer gifts and promises of protection.

 

5.Janmashtami: This festival marks the birth of Lord Krishna, and it is celebrated with devotional songs, dance performances, and reenactments of episodes from Krishna's life.


6.Ganesh Chaturthi: This festival celebrates the birth of Lord Ganesha, the elephant-headed god of wisdom and prosperity. People install clay idols of Ganesha in their homes and communities and offer prayers and sweets.


7.Makar Sankranti/Pongal: Celebrated in different parts of India, this festival marks the harvest season and the transition of the sun into the zodiac sign of Capricorn. Kite flying, feasting, and traditional sweets like tilgul are part of the celebrations.


8.Durga Puja: Particularly popular in West Bengal, Durga Puja is a celebration of the goddess Durga's victory over the demon Mahishasura. Elaborate processions, artistic displays, and cultural performances are key components of this festival.


9.Onam: Onam is a harvest festival celebrated in the state of Kerala. It includes traditional games, a grand feast (Onam Sadya), and a flower carpet (pookalam) made outside homes.


10.Maha Shivaratri: Dedicated to Lord Shiva, this festival involves fasting, night-long vigils, and temple visits. Devotees offer milk, water, and leaves to the Shiva Lingam.


11.Karva Chauth: This is a fasting ritual observed by married Hindu women for the well-being and longevity of their husbands. The fast is broken after the sighting of the moon.


12.Gudi Padwa/Ugadi: These festivals mark the Hindu New Year and are celebrated in different regions of India. People prepare special dishes and raise a Gudi (flag) in Maharashtra, while in the south, Ugadi is observed with rituals and pachadi (a special chutney).


13.Ram Navami: This festival celebrates the birth of Lord Rama and is marked by prayers, bhajans, and recitations of the Ramayana.
